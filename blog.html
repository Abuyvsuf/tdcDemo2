Event Recap

Jamhuri High School Are Kenya's 2025 Debate Champions

The 2025 TDC National Finals delivered everything a season finale should — sharp arguments, electric energy, and a champion that embodied exactly what this platform is built for.

August 5, 2025 · 4 min read

The 2025 TDC National Finals, held on August 2–3 in Nairobi, brought the full force of the season into one unforgettable weekend. Regional champions arrived after months of SDG-framed debates, ready to test not only their speaking ability but their command of Kenya’s most urgent civic and economic questions. Jamhuri High School rose through the rounds with clarity, discipline, and teamwork, eventually emerging as Kenya’s 2025 TDC National Debate Champions. The win represented more than a trophy. It affirmed the power of consistent school-based preparation, mentorship, and courageous student voices. The finals also reflected the strength of TDC’s partner ecosystem. KDIC, KBA, and AKI helped anchor a season where financial literacy was treated as a national development issue rather than a side topic. Students argued motions on savings culture, insurance awareness, economic inclusion, gender equality in finance, and the role of youth in Kenya’s financial future. KDIC’s Director of Bank Resolution, David Irungu, addressed participants on why financially informed citizens are essential to national resilience. His message was simple but powerful: young people must understand the systems that protect their money, expand opportunity, and sustain trust in the economy. After the final rounds, the community gathered for the African Themed Gala Night, a celebration of culture, friendship, and excellence. The gala allowed students, teachers, alumni, and partners to close the season with joy while honoring the hard work behind every speech. For TDC, the weekend proved that debate can be competitive, civic, and deeply human at the same time.

📷 141,000 Likes and Counting: How Mount Kenya Region Went Viral
Impact Story

141,000 Likes and Counting: How Mount Kenya Region Went Viral

When TDC posted highlights from the Mount Kenya regional, 141,900 likes and 3,900 comments came flooding in from Kenya, Uganda, and Rwanda.

April 28, 2025 · 3 min read

The Mount Kenya regional in April 2025, hosted in Nyeri, became one of the clearest signs that young people are hungry for civic and intellectual content when it is presented with energy and authenticity. After TDC posted highlights from the tournament, the clips travelled far beyond the schools in attendance. More than 141,900 likes and 3,900 comments came in from Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, and beyond. The response mattered because it challenged a lazy assumption: that students only engage with entertainment online. The comments showed that audiences were excited to see teenagers arguing policy, economics, gender, climate, and development with confidence. For participating students, the viral moment was not just about visibility. It validated the hours spent preparing cases, learning rebuttal, and standing in rooms where every idea could be challenged. The tournament ran two full days of competitive rounds, with adjudication clinics supporting both students and teachers. TDC’s vision has always been to make debate exciting, accessible, and socially relevant. The Mount Kenya moment showed that when students are trusted with serious issues, they do not make civic discourse boring. They make it alive.

Why KDIC Chose Debate as a Vehicle for Financial Literacy

The most powerful financial education doesn't happen in a classroom. It happens when students are challenged to argue for it.

August 10, 2025 · 5 min read

The KDIC-TDC partnership demonstrated that financial literacy becomes more durable when students do more than receive information. Across 12 regional circuits, students debated KDIC-themed motions that pushed them to research, question, defend, and apply financial ideas. Savings culture, deposit protection, insurance awareness, gender equality in finance, digital banking, and economic inclusion became live debate topics rather than abstract textbook terms. This mattered because debate creates ownership. A student arguing for better savings habits must understand household behavior, banking trust, regulation, and youth unemployment. A student opposing a motion must still understand the underlying system well enough to challenge it. By the time teams reached the National Finals, financial literacy had become part of a broader civic vocabulary. David Irungu, KDIC’s Director of Bank Resolution, emphasized that informed citizens strengthen national development because they know how to protect their resources and participate responsibly in the economy. TDC’s SDG framework made the partnership scalable. Financial literacy connected naturally to poverty reduction, gender equality, decent work, reduced inequalities, and strong institutions. Through competitive debate, students could connect personal money choices to national resilience and global development standards.

📷 From Shy Student to Stage Champion: What Debate Does to a Young Person
Impact Story

From Shy Student to Stage Champion: What Debate Does to a Young Person

Every champion started the same way — uncertain, nervous, unsure if their voice mattered. TDC's job is to prove that it does.

June 15, 2025 · 4 min read

Every champion started somewhere quiet. Many began as shy students who avoided eye contact, spoke too softly, or believed leadership belonged to someone else. Debate changes that by giving young people repeated, structured chances to think, speak, fail, improve, and be heard. For Kenyan high school students, the transformation is visible. Confidence grows first. A student learns to stand, breathe, and deliver a point in front of peers. Then critical thinking deepens as they discover that strong opinions are not enough; claims need evidence, structure, and relevance. Academic performance often improves because debaters read more widely and learn to organize ideas under pressure. Leadership follows naturally. Students who once feared the stage become club captains, peer mentors, and voices in school assemblies. TDC’s regional-to-national ladder gives this growth a clear pathway. Learners can begin in school clubs, advance through regional qualifiers, receive adjudication feedback, and eventually compete on a national stage. The Daily Nation described TDC as turning debate into a meaningful platform where young people meet to discuss issues that matter to society. That description captures the heart of the work. Debate is not only about winning rounds. It is about proving to a young person that their voice can carry weight when it is prepared, ethical, and courageous.

Teachers at the NSE: Building Kenya's Next Generation of Debate Coaches

The Nairobi Securities Exchange is usually a place for trading floors. In 2022, it became a classroom for the teachers who will shape Kenya's next debaters.

October 20, 2022 · 3 min read

The Nairobi Securities Exchange is usually associated with markets, listings, and trading floors. In 2022, it became something different for TDC: a classroom for the teachers who would shape Kenya’s next generation of debaters. The NSE Teachers Workshop focused on a simple idea. Sustainable debate ecosystems cannot depend on one-off tournaments alone. Schools need trained educators who can run clubs, prepare teams, adjudicate rounds, and connect debate to real-world learning. The workshop gave teachers practical tools in argument structure, motion analysis, rebuttal, judging standards, and club management. The financial literacy angle made the venue especially meaningful. Teachers explored how debate could introduce students to capital markets, savings, economic citizenship, and responsible participation in Kenya’s economy. Instead of presenting financial education as a lecture, TDC framed it as a conversation students could enter, question, and own. Teachers left with stronger coaching confidence and a clearer view of how debate clubs can become year-round spaces for leadership development. The long-term vision is national: school-based debate programmes that remain active between tournaments and keep producing informed, articulate, accountable students.

Debate and the SDGs: Why Kenya's Youth Should Argue About the World's Biggest Problems

The 17 SDGs aren't just policy targets. For TDC, they are the syllabus — a framework for training young Kenyans to think clearly, argue ethically, and lead with purpose.

March 5, 2025 · 6 min read

The 17 Sustainable Development Goals are often discussed in boardrooms, policy documents, and international conferences. For TDC, they belong in school halls too. They are not just policy targets; they are a syllabus for training young Kenyans to think clearly, argue ethically, and lead with purpose. SDG-framed debate gives students relevance and stakes. A motion about the ocean economy at the Coast is not abstract to a student from Mombasa, Kilifi, or Kwale. It connects to livelihoods, tourism, climate resilience, and SDG 14. A North Rift debate on food sovereignty speaks directly to agriculture, climate change, and household stability. A national motion on financial inclusion connects savings, insurance, gender equality, and decent work to the future students will inherit. This framework also connects Kenyan youth to global conversations without removing them from local realities. Students learn that the problems in their counties are linked to debates happening across Africa and the world: inequality, public health, education, responsible institutions, and sustainable cities. Debate then becomes more than performance. It becomes civic rehearsal. Young people practice listening to opposing views, weighing evidence, naming trade-offs, and defending solutions without losing respect for one another. Kenya and Africa need a generation that can do exactly that. If students can learn to argue about the world’s biggest problems with courage and humility, they can also learn to lead communities through them.
